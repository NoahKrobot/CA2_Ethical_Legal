Data Collection & Consent

“Processing personal data is generally prohibited, unless it is expressly allowed by law, or the data subject has consented to the processing.”

An organization can collect and process (store, analyze, aggregate, share, sell, etc.) the personal data of residents in the EU. If it does so, it is obligated to comply with GDPR.

Consent must be freely given, specifically informed and unambiguous. In order to obtain freely given consent, it must be given on a voluntary basis.

Privacy Policy

Purpose



The purpose of privacy policy is to inform data subjects at or before the point of data collection:

  • How their data is collected and how long it’s retained
  • How long their data is retained
  • The purposes for which their data is collected
  • If and how their data is sold

“Remember, simplicity is key – make it easy for website visitors to understand.”



Do

  • Make it accessible – privacy notice should be easily navigated to from anywhere on the website, so ideally place it in the footer
  • Use a layered approach with different levels of information, from specific to general
  • Use different information for different processes, collection methods, products and services
  • Give it before data is collected

    • Further reading:
    Privacy Policy for the EU

Don't

  • Make the wording too complicated
  • Hide privacy notice within terms and conditions that are difficult to find or navigate
  • Use a generic one or another organisation’s privacy notice
  • Make the privacy policy unnecessary long

Cookies

What are cookies?



Cookies are a small text files which help track user behavior, remember login details, and maintain session information, enabling a personalized browsing experience.

Fun fact - Why are internet cookies named cookies?
They’re called “cookies” because the idea comes from the old Unix OS “magic cookie” — a small piece of data passed between programs to identify a session. Internet cookies work similar: a server passes a small piece of data to the browser that it later returns so that the server can track user behavior across pages.

Why are browser cookies called cookies?


Essential Cookies



Necessary for the proper functioning of a website.

They do not process user's data.

User consent isn't required.

They are not covered by the legal basis of GDPR.






Non - Essential Cookies



Add additional functionallity not necessary for website to function.

They process, share and analyze user's data.

User consent is mandatory.

Required by GDPR.

Consent Requirements

Informed consent



Individuals must be told at minimum:

  • Who the data controller is
  • What data is collected
  • How the data will be used
  • That consent can be withdrawn at any time, and the withdrawal must be as easy as giving consent
  • The purpose of the processing (to avoid “function creep”)
What is “function creep”?
The gradual expansion of a technology or system beyond its original intended purpose, often leading to increased privacy risks. Definition reference: Dictionary.com. (2023). Dictionary.com | Meanings & Definitions of English Words. [online] Available at: https://www.dictionary.com/browse/function-creep. ‌


Unambiguous consent



Consent cannot be implied and must always be given through an opt-in, a declaration or an active motion, so that there is no misunderstanding that the data subject has consented to the particular processing

Freely given
Equally accessible and easy to use “Accept” and “Deny” options

The controller must always obtain a clear affirmative act. There must be absolutely no ambiguity about what the individual has consented to and how their data will be processed.

Personal Insight

Although there are GDPR laws on data collection and consent, many times users click through anything, often too fast to read through what they’re consenting on. This makes the idea of informed consent just a legal requirement, rather than actual protection. I believe it will become a much bigger problem once more companies start to integrate AI models (or probably more common AI chatbots) on their website. This will result in new, more complex and messier data collection policies.

Personal Insight

I remember playing a video game as a kid and seeing a privacy policy on starting it – I scrolled through it as fast as I could to play the video game. Even though the situation depicts kid’s small attention span, the same habit is applied to a majority of adults as well, including myself. How can we ask companies to be completely transparent with us if we’re not actually reading through what’s happening with our data? Again, has the privacy policy become just a part of legal checkbox? Companies definitely know that users usually do not read their policies. Is it just to complain if some consequences arise?

On the other hand, privacy policies are too long. To further prove this, I’ve read Google’s Privacy Policy found at: https://policies.google.com/privacy. To read this, it took me more than 18 minutes, and the question is how much did I genuinely remember? It is really expected of users to spend minutes on every website they visit, just to know what will happen with their data? I believe there should be more defined laws regarding the simplicity of privacy policies.
