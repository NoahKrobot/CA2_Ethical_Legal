Data Collection & Consent

“Processing personal data is generally prohibited, unless it is expressly allowed by law, or the data subject has consented to the processing.”

An organization can collect and process (store, analyze, aggregate, share, sell, etc.) the personal data of residents in the EU. If it does so, it is obligated to comply with GDPR.

Consent must be freely given, specifically informed and unambiguous. In order to obtain freely given consent, it must be given on a voluntary basis.

Privacy Policy

Purpose



The purpose of privacy policy is to inform data subjects at or before the point of data collection:

  • How their data is collected
  • How long their data is retained
  • The purposes for which their data is collected
  • If and how their data is sold

“Remember, simplicity is key – make it easy for website visitors to understand.”



Do

  • Make it accessible – privacy notice should be easily navigated to from anywhere on the website - ideally placed in the footer
  • Must be given before data is collected
  • Inform users about both general and specific information about the website
  • Share detailed and different information for different processes, collection methods, products and services

    • Further reading:
    Privacy Policy for the EU

Don't

  • Make the wording too complicated
  • Hide privacy notice within terms and conditions that are difficult to find or navigate
  • Use a generic one or another organisation’s privacy notice
  • Make the privacy policy unnecessary long

    7 Mistakes You're Making With Your Privacy Policy

Cookies

What are cookies?



Cookies are a small text files which help track user behavior, remember login details, and maintain session information, enabling a personalized browsing experience.

Fun fact - Why are internet cookies named cookies?
They’re called “cookies” because the idea comes from the old Unix OS “magic cookie” — a small piece of data passed between programs to identify a session. Internet cookies work similar: a server passes a small piece of data to the browser that it later returns so that the server can track user behavior across pages.

Further reading:
Why are browser cookies called cookies?


Essential Cookies



Necessary for the proper functioning of a website.

They do not process user's data.

User consent isn't required.

They are not covered by the legal basis of GDPR.





Non-Essential Cookies



Additional functionallity not necessary for website to function.

They process, share and analyze user's data.

User consent is mandatory.

Required by GDPR.

Valid Cookie Banner Buttons

A valid cookie banner must have four clearly visible and intuitive buttons. Each one has a specific role:



Accept

Accept all cookies.

Reject

Refuse all non-essential cookies.

Customize

Let the user pick what they accept.

Close or "X"

Banner closes and cookies stay blocked.

Data Security & Security Incidents



Imagine a dentist’s office - it is available during working hours. It holds a degree of availability

Next, all the patient’s accurate data is safely secured in their locker only authorized personnel can access. The office hold a degree of confidentiality and integrity.

A website is no different than that.
Confidentiality is achieved through protecting data subject’s sensitive information such as account passwords and payment card details from unauthorized subjects.
Integrity is achieved through ensuring the accuracy of stored information, remaining trustworthy by protecting data from being falsely altered.
Availability is achieved through keeping the website resilient (e.g. with backups and protections) against attacks or outages.


All this falls into data protection - the fundamental right under EU law. The website must clearly explain these measures in data protection compliance, a seperate document from privacy policy.

Roach Motel

What is it?



This dark pattern makes it easy for users to enter a situation that is difficult and unintuitive to get out.

Example



Signing up for a subscription is easy, but cancelling it requires navigating through multiple pages, verifications and more than one popup or confirmations buttons.

Personally, the very first time cancelling Amazon Prime subscription was difficult. I was completely lost on which steps to take, and what to click. I relied on ChatGPT to help me find the 'cancel subscription' button.

Confirmshaming

What is it?



By using guilt and shame, web designers are trying to convince users to take a desired action.

Example



For example, a fitness website offers users do decline or accept a weekly email with deals on the sign-up step. Instead of simply giving them a “Decline” option, they’d write it like: "I don’t want to receive fantastic deals to save money and increase my training gains” .

Hidden cost

What is it?



They occur when users only discover additional charges at the very last step of the purchase.

CONCEPTUAL EXAMPLE



A web shop might give a new user a welcome coupon saying the first purchase will bring 30% discount and free shipping. User would fill in their basket and would be given a price of €33 and free shipping, showing the discount is applied. However, on the very last step before paying the price would be higher, and the shipping wouldn’t be free – just like the discount was never offered and applied.

Misdirection

What is it?



Misdirection is when the design purposefully focusses user's attention on one thing in order to distract their attention from another.

Example



By looking at the next screen, it is easier to notice a red button designed to get users to upgrade. A must less noticeable grey button must be selected to reject the offer and continue checking in.


Color Red

How can a color be a dark pattern?



It has been proven that color red raises heart rate and is easier to notice.

Examples



A social media might withold the notifications of user's new post likes. This will give the user feeling of anticipation and unease. When the notifications are finally sent (or a red dot is visible on the app or website), the dopamine rush is greater, and posting becomes more addictive.

From personal experience, I've noticed TikTok showing a red dot on the mobile phone. When I'd enter the app, I'd find myself staring at no notifications - almost like they were calling me just to scroll on their app.

Privacy Zuckering

What is it?



It is a design which tricks users into sharing more personal details than they intended to.

Example



"Facebook and Google’s business model is such that they provide free services but monetise user data instead."

In more detail, a social media platform can pre-select sharing settings to make user posts public by default. To make their content private, users must manually adjust the settings.

Consent Requirements

Informed consent



Individuals must be told at minimum:

  • who the data controller is
  • what data is collected
  • how the data will be used
  • the purpose of the data processing (to avoid “function creep”)
  • that consent can be withdrawn at any time, and it will be as easy as giving consent
What is “function creep”?
The gradual expansion of a technology or system beyond its original intended purpose, often leading to increased privacy risks. Definition reference: Dictionary.com. (2023). Dictionary.com | Meanings & Definitions of English Words. [online] Available at: https://www.dictionary.com/browse/function-creep. ‌


Unambiguous consent



Consent cannot be implied and must always be given through an opt-in, a declaration or an active motion, so that there is no misunderstanding that the data subject has consented to the particular processing

Freely given
Equally accessible and easy to use “Accept” and “Deny” options.

The controller must always obtain a clear affirmative act. There must be absolutely no ambiguity about what the individual has consented to and how their data will be processed.

Security Incidents

The communication to the affected individuals must at least include



  • The name and contact details of the data protection officer or other contact where more information can be obtained or requested
  • A description of consequences of the personal data breach likely to happen
  • A description of the measures taken or proposed to be taken by the organisation to address the personal data breach "including, where appropriate, measures to mitigate its possible adverse effects"
  • Recommendations for the individuals concerned to mitigate potential adverse effects of the breach

Essential Cookies

Session cookies



They keep data subject's logged in and remember their browsing actions.

User-input cookies



They remember what data subject enter, like form answers or shopping-cart items.

User-centric security cookies



They track and detect repeated login errors to prevent abuse.

Load-balancing cookies



They distribute traffic between servers to keep the site running smoothly.

Personal Insight

Although there are GDPR laws on data collection and consent, many times users click through shown data collection policies, often too fast to read through what they’re consenting on. This makes the idea of informed consent just a legal requirement, rather than actual protection.

I also believe this topic will become a much bigger problem once more companies start to integrate AI models (or more common - AI chatbots) on their website. This will result in new, more complex and messier data collection policies, which users will just quickly skip through.

In conclusion, it would make the most sense to fix the current issues with data collection policy formats before making the topic more complicated and messier.
